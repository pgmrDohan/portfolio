I was fortunate enough to join the [IAMROOT kernel study](https://www.iamroot.org/), which I had hoped to apply for since middle school, as part of the 23rd.

During this orientation, I learned about the common rules of the IAMROOT study, and I thought they would be helpful when running or leading a study in the future, so I am writing them down.

There are a great many rules, enough to have been maintained for 23 years, but I think they are necessary for the study to endure for so long.

- Use respectful language with one another (engage in discussion based on mutual respect, regardless of age, rank, position, or education)
- Never put pressure on individuals (the moment pressure appears, the study becomes uninteresting)
  - Dividing up the chapters of a book and then having each person present later
  - Insisting on difficult books for no good reason
  - Having each person research something and present it
  - Having only one person keep source-driving all the time
  - The best study = a study that is fun and makes it easy to gain something from it, even if you come without any preparation
- Do not arbitrarily change the study time
- Preserve the purity of the study (the study is a place for learning)
- Do not try to become too close too quickly
- Do not wait for people who are late (and do not overaccommodate someone who has to miss a week for personal reasons)

Below are tips from previous riders.

- At first, trying to know everything makes things even harder
  - There is no one who can simply give you the answer, and even if there were such a person, it would be difficult to truly make it your own
- When something piques your curiosity, ask as soon as possible
  - If you let it pass, there may not be as many chances as you think to discuss that topic in depth again
- Rather than simply accepting things, it is better to think about why they are that way
- Surprisingly, books can also contain mistakes
  - Being able to find typos or incorrectly calculated numbers is proof that you are following the study well
- Keep in mind that everyone comes from different backgrounds

---

At first, I did not think I would write them, but the rule "do not put pressure on individuals" strongly resonated with me.

Currently, while serving as an operations member for [Like-Lion](https://likelion.university/) at school, I am running a study group by forming a TFT (Task Force Team) for the regular ideathons and hackathons held by the headquarters.

As I took on the role of leading the study, I had been planning things with the vague belief that having each person research and present something would be helpful for learning.

After listening to this orientation, I was able to think more about the direction of the study, and as a result, I would like to somewhat change the planning direction of the study.
